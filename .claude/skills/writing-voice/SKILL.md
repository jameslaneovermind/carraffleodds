# Skill: writing-voice

The voice is the moat. In a niche full of scammy operators and AI slop, sounding like a real, honest car person is what earns trust, ranks, and gets cited. Match it on everything public.

## The voice in one line
Honest, plain-spoken, data-driven, a bit of genuine car enthusiasm. A knowledgeable mate who's done the maths — not a marketer, not a gambling site, not a thought-leader.

## Reference standard
The live `/how-it-works` and `/about` pages are the benchmark. When in doubt, read them and match.

## Rules
- **Lead with the answer.** Open each section with the direct answer, then explain. (Also helps AI citation.)
- **Plain English.** Short sentences. No hype words ("ultimate", "game-changing", "unlock"), no filler, no "in today's fast-paced world" openers.
- **Honest about everything** — the affiliate model, that the odds are against you, that winning is a bonus not a plan. Transparency is the brand.
- **Show the maths.** Use real numbers and our value/EV/odds framing. Specifics beat adjectives.
- **Responsible by default.** Keep 18+ and responsible-play framing; never imply entering fixes money problems.
- **No fake urgency.** Don't manufacture scarcity (COMPLIANCE.md).
- **British.** UK spelling, £, UK terms.

## Formatting
Prose-first. Headings and the occasional short list where they genuinely aid scanning. Tabular numbers for data. Don't over-format.

## AI tells — find and remove

Run this audit on every draft. These patterns, *in density*, are the giveaway; one instance is fine, a stack is fatal. (The examples below illustrate the linguistic pattern — the fix matters more than the specific topic.)

1. **The reversal**: "It's not X. It's Y." / "What disappeared isn't review. It's..." / "was never X. It was Y." The single most recognisable tell. Rewrite as active statements: "We didn't remove review. We removed the renegotiation of intent..."
2. **The chiasmus/aphorism**: "Code got cheap, but the review didn't." Bumper-sticker cadence. Unpack into a plain sentence: "producing code got fast and cheap while the review process is still the one we designed for code humans typed."
3. **Anadiplosis pivot**: "...that's not the interesting part. The interesting part is..." Cut the repeat.
4. **Stacked triads**: "the approach, the blast radius, the failure modes". One triad per section max; break others into uneven shapes ("on the approach and its blast radius, and catches the things I didn't know I didn't know").
5. **Em-dash density**: more than one per paragraph reads generated. Swap for commas, parentheses, or full stops.
6. **Meme openers**: "I'll say the quiet part", "Let that sink in", "Here's the thing".
7. **Italicised emphasis word**: "They're reviewing my *thinking*". Remove the italics or rewrite.
8. **Dramatised hypothetical opener**: "Somewhere in your company right now, an engineer is staring at...". Ground it in the reader instead: "If your team uses coding agents, you've probably watched..."
9. **Stock vocabulary**: "load-bearing", "deeper lesson", "crucially", "the real question is no longer X but Y", "quiet tax". One is borderline; several is a signature.
10. **The crescendo ending**: three short aphorisms stacked in the close. Endings deserve the plainest register in the piece — fold the punches into flowing sentences and keep at most one.
11. **The dramatic fragment**: a sentence broken off mid-thought and given its own line for emphasis ("The pause is the moment." / "That was the moment." / "Everything changed."). AI manufacturing gravitas. Either complete the thought or absorb it into the surrounding paragraph.
