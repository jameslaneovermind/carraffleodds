# Research Prompt: "How one person with AI can do the job of a whole marketing team in 2026"

> Paste this into a deep-research agent (or hand it back to Claude to run). It's written to produce an actionable 2026 playbook, not a think-piece.

---

## Role & objective

You are a senior growth/marketing operator who has personally run lean, AI-leveraged growth for early-stage consumer web products. Produce a **practical, up-to-date (2026) playbook** answering one question: **how can a single non-specialist operator, using AI tools, replicate the output of a full marketing team** — and which of those tactics are actually worth doing.

This is for a real project, so be concrete and honest. Prioritise *what works now* over what sounds impressive. Where a tactic is overhyped, low-ROI, or a good way to get banned, say so plainly.

## Hard constraints to design around (do not ignore these)

- **Solo operator, ~1 hour/day**, no team, no agency.
- **Near-zero budget** (assume <£50/month for tools to start; flag where spending more is genuinely worth it).
- **The product is a UK consumer comparison/aggregator site in a gambling-adjacent niche** (UK prize-draw / "win a car" competitions). This matters: most paid ad channels (Google Ads, Meta) heavily restrict gambling-adjacent advertising and affiliate content, so **assume paid acquisition is mostly off the table** and weight the research toward **organic, earned, owned, and community channels**. Call out explicitly which channels are blocked or risky for this niche.
- Operator is technical (can build/script with AI coding tools) but is *not* a marketing specialist — so favour workflows a capable generalist can run, and explain the marketing reasoning, not just the steps.

## What to cover

**1. The team-replacement map.** List the functions a traditional marketing team performs (SEO, content production, social/community management, PR & outreach/link-building, email/CRM lifecycle, paid acquisition, analytics/measurement, brand & creative, partnerships/affiliate, competitive intelligence). For each: the 2026 AI-enabled way a solo operator does it, the specific current tools, the realistic time cost per week, and an honest ROI/priority rating for a tiny site. Be clear about which functions a solo operator should simply *skip*.

**2. Community & Reddit (deep section — the operator specifically wants this).**
- How to find relevant conversations at scale across Reddit, niche forums, Facebook groups, Discord, and (where relevant) X/TikTok comments — including current keyword-monitoring/social-listening tools (e.g. things like GummySearch, Syften, F5Bot, Brand24, Notify, and any 2026 newcomers), what they cost, and what they actually surface.
- The **current (2026) state of Reddit's API, rate limits, scraping rules, and ToS** — what's allowed, what gets you blocked, and the realistic options for monitoring vs. data scraping.
- **The authentic-participation vs. astroturfing line:** what genuinely works on Reddit/forums in 2026 without getting shadowbanned, downvoted into oblivion, or damaging the brand — account warming, value-first commenting, mod relationships, when/whether to disclose affiliation, and the disclosure rules (FTC/ASA) that apply. Be specific about what *fails* (obvious AI comments, drive-by link drops, fake accounts) and why.
- Where AI helps (drafting, summarising threads, prioritising which conversations are worth a human reply) vs. where AI use is a liability (auto-posting, mass commenting). Include realistic time-saving workflows.

**3. AEO / GEO — getting discovered through AI, not just Google.** The current 2026 picture on optimising to be **cited by ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews / AI Mode, and Gemini**. What concretely drives AI citations now (structured data, comparison tables, freshness, entity/brand mentions, presence on sources LLMs trust like Reddit), how to measure AI referral traffic and citation share, and the current tools for tracking it. Treat this as a first-class channel, not a footnote.

**4. Programmatic & AI-assisted content/SEO in 2026.** How solo operators use AI to produce content and programmatic pages at scale *without* getting hit by Google's spam/"scaled content" updates — the current line between useful programmatic pages and demoted AI slop, what Google's 2025–2026 guidance and core updates actually penalise, and the data/quality bar a page must clear. Include the current AI content/SEO tool landscape (and which are worth it).

**5. Short-form video & creative with AI.** The 2026 state of faceless/short-form video, AI avatars, AI voiceover, and AI image/video generation for organic social (TikTok/Reels/Shorts) — current tools, what quality is achievable, what audiences and platforms now penalise as obvious AI, and whether this is worth an hour-a-day operator's time.

**6. Automation & agent stacks.** The current (2026) practical automation stack a solo operator uses to glue this together — e.g. n8n / Make / Zapier, browser-automation agents, and LLM-in-the-loop workflows for monitoring, drafting, scheduling, and reporting. Give 3–5 concrete example workflows with the tools named. Flag reliability/maintenance cost and where automation breaks.

**7. Email & owned audience.** 2026 best practice for building and monetising an email list on a tiny budget — capture tactics, current ESP options at free/cheap tiers, deliverability realities, and AI's role in lifecycle/content. Cover GDPR/PECR (UK) compliance for capture and any scraped/cold contact.

**8. The honest tool stack & budget.** A consolidated, current (2026) recommended tool stack for the whole operation with real pricing, organised by priority (free/essential → worth-paying-for → skip). Note tools that have died, pivoted, or been disrupted by AI recently so the operator isn't chasing dead recommendations.

**9. Legal/ethical guardrails.** A clear summary of the rules that bite: platform ToS (Reddit, Meta, TikTok), FTC/ASA disclosure for affiliate and sponsored content, GDPR/PECR for scraping and email, and gambling-adjacent advertising restrictions. Frame as "here's how to stay on the right side of this," not a disclaimer.

**10. The prioritised 1-hour-a-day plan.** Synthesise everything into a ruthless priority stack: if you only have an hour a day, what do you do, in what order, and what do you deliberately ignore? Include a realistic week-in-the-life and the metrics that tell you it's working.

## Recency & quality requirements

- **Heavily weight 2026 sources.** Treat anything from 2024 or earlier as potentially stale, and say so when you rely on it. AI marketing tooling and platform rules change monthly — flag where your information may already be out of date and what the operator should re-verify.
- Cite sources for tool capabilities, pricing, platform rules, and any statistics.
- Prefer primary sources (platform docs/ToS, tool pricing pages, Google Search Central guidance, regulator pages) over listicles and affiliate "best tools" roundups, which are often paid placements — and note when a recommendation might be biased.
- Be specific and current with tool names, prices, and limits. Avoid vague advice ("create great content") — give the actual workflow.
- Where you're estimating or extrapolating, label it clearly.

## Output format

A structured playbook with: a one-paragraph honest verdict up top (can one person + AI really replace a marketing team, and where does that claim break down?), the sections above, a consolidated tool stack table, and the prioritised 1-hour-a-day plan at the end. Direct and pragmatic — no fluff, no thought-leader posturing.
