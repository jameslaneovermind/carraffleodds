UK car competitions are legitimate — in the sense that most of them are legally run, real prizes get delivered, and real people win them. That said, the market is unregulated (no Gambling Commission licence required), standards vary considerably between operators, and a handful of sites have raised legitimate concerns. This guide covers the key questions, with links to the detail.

## Are they legal?

Yes. UK car competitions operate legally under the Gambling Act 2005 as prize draws — not lotteries, not gambling. The mechanism is a free entry route (anyone can enter by post at no cost) or a genuine skill question. That removes the element that would otherwise make them require a Gambling Commission licence.

The government keeps this under review. DCMS published a report in June 2025 recommending Gambling Commission regulation for the sector. No legislation has passed yet, but it's a live policy question. For more detail on how the law actually works: [Are UK Car Competitions Legal?](/guides/are-car-competitions-legal)

## Can you enter for free?

Yes, for most competitions. Operators who charge for tickets must accept a free postal entry — a handwritten postcard sent to their address — with equal odds to paid entries. The main exception is BOTB's flagship Spot the Ball competition, which is a skill competition and therefore exempt from the free entry requirement.

In practice, the volume of postal entries is low enough that some entrants find it competitive. Full details on how to do it properly: [How to Enter Car Competitions for Free](/guides/free-entry-car-competitions)

## How do you spot a legitimate site?

The core checks are: Companies House registration (active company, trading for a reasonable period), Trustpilot score with a meaningful review count (not just a handful), evidence of real live draws or draw recordings, and a published winner list with names.

The DCMS Voluntary Code of Good Practice — launched May 2026 — is also a useful filter. Signatories commit to transparent draws, responsible gambling measures, and accurate marketing. Not all operators have signed it. The breakdown of which sites are signatories is in each of our [site reviews](/sites). More on what to look for: [Are Car Competitions a Scam? ## Do people actually win?

Yes — and for most of the established operators, there's substantial evidence. Rev Comps has over 6,300 publicly named vehicle winners with photos and draw records. BOTB publishes weekly PromoVeritas-verified results. Elite Competitions and Dream Car Giveaways stream live draws on Facebook and YouTube so you can watch the result happen.

The key is knowing what to look for when verifying: [Do People Actually Win Car Competitions?](/guides/do-people-actually-win)

## What happens when you win?

You'll typically be contacted by phone or email within minutes to a few hours of the draw. You'll need to provide photo ID and proof of address. Most operators offer a cash alternative (usually at a discount to the car's headline value) — in practice, most winners take the cash.

Prize winnings from UK competitions are tax-free. Car delivery timelines vary; cash prizes can arrive in hours with the faster operators. Full breakdown: [What Happens When You Win a Car Competition?](/guides/what-happens-when-you-win)

## The honest summary

Legitimate operators exist, real prizes get delivered, and the legal structure is sound. The main risks are: spending more than you planned (the habit-forming nature of weekly draws is a recurring forum concern), entering operators who lack transparency, and misunderstanding the odds. A £1 ticket in a 10,000-ticket draw is 1 in 10,000. Treat it as entertainment, not a financial strategy.
