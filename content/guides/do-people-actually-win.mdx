Yes — and for most of the established operators, there's substantial, verifiable evidence. Rev Comps has published over 6,300 named vehicle winners with photos and draw records. BOTB sends an independent verification letter with every weekly result. Elite Competitions and Dream Car Giveaways stream live draws that anyone can watch. The question isn't whether people win; it's how to verify that what an operator claims is real.

## How to evaluate winner evidence

Not all "proof" is equal. Here's the spectrum from most to least verifiable:

**Most verifiable:**
- Live draw recording where the ticket number is selected on-screen and visible
- Independent verification by a third party (PromoVeritas, an inspection company)
- Named winner + photo + draw recording linked together
- Physical draw mechanism (ball machine) on camera

**Moderately verifiable:**
- Named winner and photo without a linked draw recording
- Winners page with names but no draw footage
- Social media posts from winners (self-reported, not operator-verified)

**Least verifiable:**
- Operator announcement without a name or visual evidence
- "Winner testimonials" that could be fabricated
- Prize car photos without a named winner

When you're evaluating a site, look for where their winner evidence sits on that spectrum.

## What a live draw actually shows

A live draw is a video (on Facebook, YouTube, or Instagram) where the operator selects the winning ticket in real time, with the draw mechanism visible. The two most common mechanisms are:

**Google RNG:** Google's website has a random number generator at google.com/search — type "random number generator" with your range. The operator inputs the total ticket count, generates a number, and that's the winner. The advantage is that it's publicly accessible and independently verifiable — anyone can replicate the result isn't possible (it's truly random each time), but anyone watching can confirm the process is genuine.

**Physical ball machine:** Rev Comps uses an actual lottery-style ball machine. Numbered balls are loaded for each active ticket in the draw, the machine tumbles, and a ball is drawn. This is more theatre than the Google RNG but also more intuitively trustworthy — it's physically harder to rig than a digital display.

After the number is drawn, the operator (or a computer system) matches it to the ticket holder's name and contacts them. The draw recording stays posted publicly.

## What PromoVeritas does

BOTB uses PromoVeritas — an independent prize promotion verification company — for its Spot the Ball results. PromoVeritas attends the judging process and certifies the outcome. When BOTB announces a winner, the announcement includes a letter from PromoVeritas confirming the result is independently verified.

This is the gold standard for this sector. PromoVeritas's involvement means a third party with no stake in the outcome has confirmed the process was run correctly. No other operator in our dataset currently uses a similar independent verification body for their draws.

## The repeat winner question

It's mathematically possible for the same person to win more than once if draws are genuinely independent. If a draw has 10,000 tickets and runs weekly, someone buying 100 tickets per week will win about once every 100 draws, or roughly once every two years. In a market of millions of active entrants, some people will win multiple times by chance.

When a repeat winner is questioned, the right response from an operator is to demonstrate that each draw was independently conducted and verifiable — not to suppress the scrutiny. A site that flags critical questions about repeat wins as "illegal content" (rather than addressing them with evidence) is a warning sign about how they handle accountability generally.

## How to check a specific draw result

1. Find the draw recording — search the operator's Facebook or YouTube page for the competition name or draw date
2. Note the winning ticket number from the recording
3. Check the operator's website — most have a "verify your ticket" function or a winner announcement tied to that draw
4. If the operator publishes draw reports or certificates, those are the most direct verification

If you can't find any of these, the operator has a transparency problem worth noting.
